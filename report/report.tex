\section{The rule of cartesian coordinates for $n$-D regular simplex}

The cartesian coordinates of a $n$-D regular simplex in
$\bbR^n$ can be obtained from these two
properties\,\cite{simplexWiki}
\begin{enumerate}
	\item For a regular simplex, the distances of its vertices to its center are equal.
	\item The angle subtended by any two vertices of ann-dimensional simplex through its center is
		$\arccos(-1/n)$.
\end{enumerate}

For example in 2D, the vectors $(v_0, v_1, v_2)$ are the vertics
of a 2-simplex or regular triangle. Write these as 
\begin{gather*}
	\begin{pmatrix}
		x_0 \\ y_0 
	\end{pmatrix}, ~
	\begin{pmatrix}
		x_1 \\ y_1 
	\end{pmatrix}, ~
	\begin{pmatrix}
		x_2 \\ y_2 
	\end{pmatrix}.
\end{gather*}
Without loss of generality, one can always choose the first vector
$v_0=(1,0)^T$. Then the vectors become 
\begin{gather*}
	\begin{pmatrix}
		1 \\ 0
	\end{pmatrix}, ~
	\begin{pmatrix}
		x_1 \\ y_1 
	\end{pmatrix}, ~
	\begin{pmatrix}
		x_2 \\ y_2 
	\end{pmatrix}.
\end{gather*}
By the second property the dot product of $v_0$ with all other
vectors is $-1/2$, so each of their $x$ components must equal
this, and the vectors become 
\begin{gather*}
	\begin{pmatrix}
		1 \\ 0
	\end{pmatrix}, ~
	\begin{pmatrix}
		-1/2 \\ y_1 
	\end{pmatrix}, ~
	\begin{pmatrix}
		-1/2 \\ y_2 
	\end{pmatrix}.
\end{gather*}
Next by the first property, the length of all vectors $v_j$
must be equal, therefore the possible square roots give the two
results 
\begin{gather*}
	\begin{pmatrix}
		1 \\ 0
	\end{pmatrix}, ~
	\begin{pmatrix}
		-1/2 \\ \sqrt{3}/2
	\end{pmatrix}, ~
	\begin{pmatrix}
		-1/2 \\ -\sqrt{3}/2
	\end{pmatrix}.
\end{gather*}

The process can be carried out in $n$ dimension, using $n+1$
vectors, applying the first and second properties alternately to
determine cartesian coordinates of $n$-D regular simplex in $\bbR^n$.
The following algorithm is to achieve the above process.

\begin{algorithm}
	\caption{n-D regular simplex coordinates (MATLAB style)} 
\begin{algorithmic}
	\STATE Given $x(1:n, 1:n+1) = 0$
	\FOR {$i=1:1:n$}
	\STATE $x(i,i)=\sqrt{1-\sum_{k=1}^{i-1} [x(k, i)]^{2}}$
		\FOR {$j=i+1:1:n+1$}
		\STATE $x(i,j)
		=\dfrac{1}{x(i,i)}\Big(-\frac{1}{n}-x(1:i-1, i)^T \cdot
		x(1:i-1, j)\Big)$
		\ENDFOR
	\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Algorithm Implementation}

\subsection{Ackley function}
\begin{align*}
	f(\textbf{x}) = f(x_1, \cdots, x_n)=
	-a\exp\left(-b\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i^2}\right)-\exp\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\cos(cx_i)\right)+
	a + \exp(1)
\end{align*}
$n$ is the dimension, where $a=20$, $b=0.2$ and $c=2\pi$.
Ackley function has many local minima
and a unique global minimum of $(0,0)$ with $f(0,0)=0$.

\textbf{Setup:} 

Search region: $(-10, 10)^n$; initial position: random;
$N_{max}=2^m(n+1)$, $m=5$. The maximum
number of ration is $2^m = 32$. The convergent criterion is
$\rho<1.0^{-5}$.
